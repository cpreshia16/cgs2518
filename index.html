Blackness & Superhero Media

Representation Matters

With all of the negative headlines concerning race relations it would seem like we are not living in a post-racial society like so many people would claim. Race in the world, especially in the United States is prominent and divisive. Black people and many other people of color have been dealing with that reality for centuries. Something that contributes to the divisive nature of race within the United States is underrepresentation, and misrepresentation of racial minorities. The same can also be said about gender, and sex in our current society.

Although many archaic notions of the past have been eliminated, there still exists certain negative gender stereotypes and roles that are pervasive in our society. Now, think of five superheroes! You probably thought of heroes like Batman, Superman, Spider-Man, Captain America, Thor, etc.

The powers and abilities of these superheroes you thought of are probably diverse, but that’s where their diversity ends. A majority of the superheroes you thought of are probably men, and most likely all of them white. Now, why is this? It’s due to cultural assumptions that have been ingrained into the societal unconscious. The white male has been culturally set as the default to many members of American society. While this stems from prejudice, stereotypes, sexism, and racism this behavior is influenced by the media that we consume. Media helps to shape our reality.

According to Oscar Wilde, in his essay The Decay of Lying,
"...life imitates Art far more than Art imitates life. This results not merely from Life’s imitative instinct, but from the fact that the self-conscious aim of Life is to find expression, and that Art offers it certain beautiful forms through which it may realize that energy.”

In this particular case life truly does imitate art. Our actions and our prejudices are directly influenced by who is represented in the media that we consume. Although black representation within media has increased and become less stereotypical over the years it is still an issue that is prevalent especially within the modern superhero genre.
